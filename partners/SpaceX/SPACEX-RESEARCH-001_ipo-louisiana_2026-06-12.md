# SpaceX Deep Dive: The Record IPO, the Louisiana Play, and Where a Lafayette Power/Cooling/Construction Firm Fits

- **The IPO is real and historic.** SpaceX (the parent company, not a Starlink spinoff) priced at $135/share on June 11 and began trading June 12, 2026 on Nasdaq under ticker **SPCX**, raising ~$75 billion at a ~$1.77 trillion IPO valuation — the largest IPO in history — and opened up 11.11% at $150 (intraday high $168.75), with one tracker (TradingKey) putting intraday market cap as high as ~$2.05 trillion. Elon Musk became the world's first trillionaire.

Likely use: Starship manufacturing, Raptor engine testing, and barge logistics rather than primarily a launch pad.
- **There is a credible, specific entry path for a power/cooling/construction firm.** SpaceX's enormous capex, its behind-the-meter power playbook (via the xAI/Colossus side), its in-house data-center and orbital-compute buildout, and a potential greenfield Gulf Coast industrial site all align with modular gas microgrids, BESS, liquid cooling, and heavy industrial construction. The realistic route is Tier-1/Tier-2 subcontracting and facility/site construction — not flight hardware.

## Key Findings

**1. IPO verified.** SpaceX went public June 12, 2026. The parent entity (Space Exploration Technologies Corp.) listed — not a standalone Starlink spinoff. Ticker SPCX on Nasdaq, fixed price $135, ~555.6 million shares, ~$75B raised, ~$1.77T IPO valuation. The deal was multiple times oversubscribed, with a planned ~30% retail allocation.

**2. The S-1 exposed the financials for the first time.** 2025 revenue $18.7B (up 33% YoY from $14.1B); GAAP net loss $4.9B; adjusted EBITDA $6.58B. Starlink (Connectivity) is the only consistently profitable segment, and its profits are funding heavy losses in the newly merged xAI ("SpaceXAI") segment.

**3. Louisiana intelligence is rich but unconfirmed.** The land deal has not publicly closed; neither SpaceX nor ExxonMobil has officially confirmed it. But circumstantial evidence is heavy, and the state has rewritten its laws specifically to land the project.

**4. The partnership thesis is sound but requires aerospace-grade qualification.** SpaceX is heavily vertically integrated but outsources specialized processes, materials, and — critically — facility construction and power infrastructure.

## Details

### A. The IPO — what happened

SpaceX confidentially filed a draft registration statement with the SEC on April 1, 2026, then publicly filed its S-1 on May 20, 2026. It ran an abbreviated roadshow in early June and used an unusual take-it-or-leave-it fixed price of $135/share rather than a price range. Shares priced after market close June 11 and began trading around 11:46 a.m. ET June 12 on Nasdaq under SPCX.

- **Size & valuation:** ~555.6 million shares (NPR reported 555,555,555 shares) at $135 = ~$75B raised, ~$1.77T IPO valuation. This surpasses Saudi Aramco's 2019 record (sources conflict on Aramco's exact figure: Renaissance Capital/NPR cite ~$25.6–29.4B; TradingKey cites $35.4B — either way, SpaceX's ~$75B is more than double). Goldman Sachs was lead/top underwriter; Morgan Stanley, BofA, Citi, and JPMorgan were joint bookrunners; 21 banks were involved.
- **First-day trading:** Per TradingKey (June 12), SpaceX "opened at $150 in its market debut, up 11.11% from its offering price of $135, with trading turnover exceeding $11.4 billion," and its "latest market capitalization reached $2.05 trillion... surpassing Broadcom, Tesla, and Meta." Shares traded as high as $168.75 (+25%) and settled around $160–165. Indications had initially pointed as high as $175.
- **Index implications:** Nasdaq rule changes let a top-40-by-market-cap new listing be fast-tracked into the Nasdaq 100 within 15 trading days (~July 1, 2026), potentially forcing ~$7B in mechanical index-fund buying against a thin ~3% float. The S&P 500 will NOT immediately include it because SpaceX fails the profitability test.
- **Control:** Musk holds roughly 85% of voting power and is chairman/CEO. Public float is small (~3%). Insiders win big: COO Gwynne Shotwell and CFO Bret Johnsen hold stakes reportedly worth >$1B each (Financial Times); director Antonio Gracias' stake could ultimately be worth ~$65B; more than 4,400 current/former employees become millionaires.

### B. The S-1 financials and segments

SpaceX now reports three segments:

- **Connectivity (Starlink):** 2025 revenue $11.387B (61% of total; 69% in Q1 2026), income from operations $4.423B, segment adjusted EBITDA $7.168B (~63% margin). Q1 2026: revenue $3.257B, operating income $1.188B. Subscribers grew 2.3M (2023) → 4.4M (2024) → 8.9M (2025) → 10.3M (end of March 2026), across 164 countries, ~9,600 satellites. ARPU FELL from $99/mo (2023) to $66/mo (Q1 2026) as cheaper consumer plans expanded the base; SpaceX raised prices up to $10/mo in May 2026 to reverse this. ~75% of all active maneuverable satellites in LEO are Starlink's.
- **Space (launch/Dragon/NASA):** 2025 revenue $4.086B, operating loss $657M, adjusted EBITDA $653M; Q1 2026 revenue $619M, operating loss $662M. 165 Falcon 9 launches in 2025 (more than the rest of the world combined); only ~43 were for outside customers — nearly three-quarters were internal Starlink launches. ~$3B in R&D went to Starship development. Starship has flown ~11–12 test flights; V3 flew its maiden flight May 22, 2026.
- **AI (xAI / "SpaceXAI"):** SpaceX acquired xAI in February 2026 (all-stock; deal valued SpaceX ~$1T and xAI ~$250B, combined ~$1.25T). The AI segment LOST $6.355B in 2025 (operating) and posted a $2.469B operating loss in Q1 2026 alone — the primary driver of consolidated losses. Includes Grok, X, and Colossus compute.

Consolidated: 2025 revenue $18.674B, loss from operations $(2.589)B, adjusted EBITDA $6.584B, net loss $4.9B; Q1 2026 net loss ~$4.3B. Accumulated deficit since 2002: $41.3B.

**Total addressable market claim:** SpaceX claims a $28.5T TAM ($370B space, $1.6T connectivity, $26.5T AI) — "the largest actionable total addressable market in human history."

**Valuation skeptics:** Morningstar's discounted-cash-flow model valued SpaceX at just $780B — roughly $611B enterprise value for the combined core launch + Starlink businesses, plus only ~$170B in probability-weighted scenarios for AI. Analyst Nicolas Owens (with Suryansh Sharma) wrote: "We think the company has been significantly overvalued and investors will have opportunities to buy the stock at more attractive levels after the IPO," flagging uncertainty as "very high." Bull case: New Street Research's James Ratzer initiated at a $165 price target, arguing Starship plus V3 satellites could make capacity "explode" for Starlink.

### F. Orbital data centers, power & cooling (directly relevant to the user)

SpaceX revealed its **AI1 orbital data center satellite** ~3 days before the IPO: 150 kW peak / 120 kW sustained compute (~one Nvidia GB300 rack), 70 kW/ton density, 70 m wingspan, 250 W/m² solar arrays (made in-house at Bastrop), and **up to 110 m² of deployable liquid radiators with redundant pumping loops** for heat rejection in vacuum. It has filed FCC plans for up to **one million** AI satellites. Anchor compute customers: **Anthropic** (renting all of Colossus 1; SpaceX providing ~300 MW / 220,000+ Nvidia GPUs) and **Google** (a reported $920M/month compute deal; renting Colossus 2 capacity through June 2029; Alphabet owns ~6.1% of SpaceX).

On the terrestrial side, the xAI/Colossus complex (now under SpaceX) is the flagship behind-the-meter power story: Colossus 1 and 2 near Memphis run ~1,498 MW of gas-turbine capacity; xAI obtained a Mississippi permit for **41 natural gas turbines (1.2 GW)** at Southaven, paired with Tesla Megapacks (BESS). Per Cleanview's "Bypassing the Grid" report, ~2 GW of behind-the-meter generation was online across four projects by mid-2026, and **Caterpillar has the highest OEM market share (33%), with more than 8.8 GW of permitted Caterpillar engines** (and Solar Turbines) tracked. Houston's Solaris builds 6–35 MW mobile units. **This is the single closest analog to the user's 10 MW Cat-genset + BESS microgrid product.**

### I. Supplier / procurement model

SpaceX is heavily vertically integrated but maintains a tiered external supply chain (Tier 1 direct; Tier 2 sub-tier). It has a public "Becoming a supplier" portal at **spacex.com/supplier** and a formal Supplier Manual (Supplier Request Form SPX-00002691, Part Approval Process SPX-00034901, Warpdrive records). Supplier categories include Flight; Non-Flight (tooling, testing, calibration, ground support equipment, qualification material); General Use; and Restricted General Use. Qualification typically requires AS9100D, ISO 9001, and NADCAP (for special processes), ITAR compliance, and on-site facility audits; a Supply Chain Reliability team handles SDRs, MRB, RTV, and CAPA. There is no public approved-vendor list (most relationships are under NDA). SpaceX states it is "committed to including diverse suppliers in our sourcing process."

Relevant to the user: SpaceX actively hires **Construction Superintendents (incl. Fluid Systems / Starship Launch Pad), Facilities staff, Electricians (incl. Medium Voltage), HVAC technicians, Automation & Controls engineers, and Sourcing/Supply-Chain roles** at Starbase — confirming heavy use of construction, electrical, fluids, and facilities trades. In Texas, SpaceX is a registered property owner/developer with 14 documented construction projects (~$315M), including a $43M "Project Echo" shell building in Bastrop. It hires regional builders and fab yards rather than importing them.

### J. Louisiana — the detailed picture

**Status (as of June 12, 2026):** No land purchase has publicly closed; neither SpaceX nor ExxonMobil has officially confirmed a deal. The story is officially "rumored/under negotiation," though circumstantial evidence is heavy.

- **The land:** ~136,000 acres (~212 sq mi) of ExxonMobil-owned coastal marsh south of LA-82 near Pecan Island/Freshwater City in Vermilion Parish, managed for hunting by the Vermilion Corporation. Exxon had planned a carbon-capture project there but quietly withdrew its wetlands permit (Feb 20, 2026), leaving the tract without a path to monetization. Hunting leases were reportedly being terminated for 2026-27.
- **Key confirmation:** State Sen. Bob Hensgens (R-Abbeville) confirmed May 7, 2026 that a "space exploration company" was negotiating with ExxonMobil: "I know both companies [SpaceX and Blue Origin] are trying to find property in southwest Louisiana... I know from people in the parishes that the companies have made outreach in the area." He refused to sign an NDA: "I cannot do that to the people who elected me." SpaceX's official X account did not deny Louisiana plans (~May 12), saying it is "constantly exploring to find viable sites to expand Starship operations." Realtor Jim Keaty of Keaty Real Estate (Lafayette) reported a ~136,000-acre assemblage for a possible spaceport; a 14-acre Pecan Island tract was bought May 1 by a Brownsville SpaceX employee.
- **Related Exxon settlement:** On May 29, 2026, Gov. Jeff Landry announced a coastal-land-dispute settlement "in principle" with ExxonMobil (AG Liz Murrill cautioned documents weren't yet signed), which observers noted could clear the way for Exxon to sell the tract free and clear.
- **Incentive laws (2026 session, signed by Landry):** A coordinated 7-bill package filed in the final 48 hours before the March 31 deadline. Confirmed signed: **HB 1088 (Act 190** — sales/use tax rebate; threshold **$1B capital + 200 jobs**, window July 2026–July 2031, 20-year term extendable 10); **HB 1179 (Act 102** — extends the Industrial Tax Exemption Program / ITEP property-tax break to aerospace); **HB 1033 (Act 188** — aerospace = critical infrastructure, unauthorized entry a felony); **HB 1071 (Act 101** — exempts aerospace blueprints/technical/security data from public records for ITAR/DoD-contracted firms); and **HB 1250** (early dismissal of certain spaceport lawsuits). Liability-shield bills **HB 1098 and HB 1099** (barring noise/light/vibration nuisance and trespass claims, voiding injunctive relief) and definitional **HB 1175** were on the governor's desk / fluid as of June 11. These explicitly neutralize the legal tools South Texas residents are using against SpaceX.
- **Economic-development players:** Lafayette Economic Development Authority (LEDA, CEO Mandi Mitchell) and One Acadiana (CEO Troy Wayman) are reportedly leading local recruitment with state LED (Secretary Susan Bourgeois). Landry reportedly visited the marsh during Easter week.
- **Why Louisiana / likely use:** The site sits roughly halfway between Boca Chica and Cape Canaveral on the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway, allowing oversized Starship hardware to be barged between sites. It offers methane/LNG infrastructure (Cameron Parish terminals), an Entergy hurricane-hardened high-voltage transmission corridor, the Port of Iberia (100+ marine fabrication yards) and Acadiana Regional Airport, and a marine-fabrication workforce. Analysts diverge: Eric Berger (Ars Technica) thinks polar-orbit satellite launches are plausible ("it sits nicely looking south... you could fly a long way before you hit land"); Quilty Space's Kimberly Siversen Burke thinks Raptor engine test stands, tank farms, integration bays, and a manufacturing/barge-logistics hub are more likely than a full launch complex, given hurricane/sea-level vulnerability. NASA's Stennis Space Center (125,000-acre buffer for engine testing) is the closest analog.
- **Michoud:** NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility (New Orleans East, 832 acres, ~3,000–4,200 workers, ~20 tenants, ~$493M annual economic impact) is a turnkey aerospace manufacturing complex; Texas firm Vivace recently won a Starlab contract there. It's a potential SpaceX manufacturing entry point but is currently dominated by Boeing (SLS) and Lockheed (Orion).
- **Starlink in Louisiana:** Per Broadband Breakfast (June 11, 2026), "Louisiana has finalized its grant agreement with SpaceX under the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment program... set to serve 10,635 homes and businesses in the state for about $82 million" — separate from the land deal. Globalstar (Covington, LA) is an adjacent satellite player.

## Recommendations (for a Lafayette power/cooling/industrial-construction firm)

**Reality check first:** SpaceX will not buy flight hardware from a new general contractor. Your realistic, high-probability targets are (1) **site/facility construction and civil works**, (2) **behind-the-meter power generation + BESS**, (3) **liquid cooling / thermal management for terrestrial compute and ground infrastructure**, and (4) **Tier-2 subcontracting** to the EPCs and fab yards SpaceX will hire. Your oilfield/subsea/heavy-industrial pedigree, GC license, and Gulf Coast location are genuine advantages for a Louisiana/Gulf project specifically.

**Stage 1 — Position now (0–3 months):**
1. Register on SpaceX's supplier portal (spacex.com/supplier) under Facilities/Construction/Non-Flight categories, not flight hardware. Emphasize ground-support, power, and thermal scopes.
2. Set up USASpending.gov and SAM.gov alerts for "Space Exploration Technologies Corp" so you see any Louisiana-tied prime awards early.
3. Engage LEDA (Mandi Mitchell) and One Acadiana (Troy Wayman) directly — they are the local gatekeepers actively assembling the regional supplier ecosystem. Ask to be added to any aerospace supplier database they are building for the Vermilion project.
4. Pursue the certifications that unlock aerospace work: AS9100D (for any hardware-adjacent scopes), ISO 9001, and a documented ITAR compliance program. For pure construction/power, ITAR plus a strong safety/quality record matters most.

**Stage 2 — Build the beachhead (3–12 months):**
5. Target the EPC/fab-yard primes who will actually build a Vermilion site — Cajun Industries, Turner Industries (New Iberia yard), Patriot Construction (Port of Iberia marine), and peers — as Tier-1s you subcontract under. SpaceX historically hires regional builders and fab yards rather than importing them.
6. Lead with your differentiated product: **10 MW modular gas microgrid nodes (Cat gensets + BESS)** map directly onto SpaceX/xAI's proven behind-the-meter playbook — Caterpillar is the #1 OEM (33% share) in this exact market. A remote Vermilion greenfield with no fast grid interconnect is the textbook behind-the-meter use case; pitch speed-to-power.
7. Pitch **liquid cooling (CDUs / thermal management)** for any terrestrial compute, Starlink gateway, or integration/test facility — and position as a domestic supplier conversant in the same liquid-radiator/pumping-loop challenges SpaceX is solving for AI1.
8. Pursue Starlink ground-segment power: gateways need gensets + BESS + power conditioning. These are smaller, faster wins than the megaproject and build a track record.

**Stage 3 — Scale with the project (12+ months):**
9. If the Vermilion deal closes, compete for site civil works, tank farms, power distribution, medium-voltage electrical, fluids systems, and HVAC — the exact trades SpaceX is hiring for at Starbase.
10. Explore Michoud as a parallel manufacturing-tenant entry point.

**Benchmarks that should change your strategy:**
- **If SpaceX officially confirms the Vermilion purchase** → accelerate hard; staff up for a multi-year megaproject and lock in LEDA/One Acadiana relationships immediately.
- **If the deal is for engine testing/manufacturing (not launch)** → power and cooling demand is steadier and larger; lean into microgrid + thermal.
- **If the deal stalls or moves to a different parish/state** → pivot the same capabilities to the broader Gulf Coast behind-the-meter data-center boom (e.g., Meta's ~2 GW Louisiana cluster), which is real regardless of SpaceX.

## Caveats
- **The Louisiana land deal is NOT officially confirmed.** It rests on one on-record state senator, anonymous sources, a realtor's blog, and circumstantial legislative/lease activity. SpaceX has only said it is "exploring" sites. Treat any "confirmed Louisiana spaceport" claim (e.g., from fan wikis) as speculative. The new public-records exemption (HB 1071/Act 101) and NDAs will keep details opaque.
- **IPO figures are first-day/intraday.** The $150 open and $168.75 intraday high are from June 12 trading; the final settled close may differ. The ~$1.77T figure is the IPO-price valuation; TradingKey's ~$2.05T is an intraday market-cap reading — treat the higher number as preliminary.
- **Financial sourcing varies.** Pre-S-1 estimates ($15–16B revenue, $8B EBITDA) circulated widely; the S-1 figures ($18.7B revenue, $6.58B EBITDA, $4.9B net loss) supersede them. Some secondary outlets blend Starlink-only vs. consolidated numbers — I've used the SEC S-1 where possible. The Saudi Aramco comparison figure is itself reported inconsistently (~$25.6B to $35.4B).
- **xAI merger dominates the loss picture.** SpaceX's core space+Starlink business may be operationally profitable; the headline net loss is largely xAI/compute capex.
- **Project-type uncertainty.** Whether Vermilion becomes a launch site, an engine-test/manufacturing hub, or a logistics node materially changes the power/cooling/construction opportunity mix.
- **Supplier process is opaque and ITAR-gated.** No public vendor list exists; most work flows through NDAs and certified primes. Expect a long qualification cycle.
